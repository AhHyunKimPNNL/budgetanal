    Macro-microphysics subcycling

    motivation

    • Wan et al. (2021) mentioned that increasing the subcycling frequency causes the cooling, drying, and cloud fraction decrease in the tropical middle and upper troposphere and mostly high-cloud fractions.
    • time step sensitivity due to MAC-MIC coupling is expected to be mostly due to the microphysics.
    • However, Santos et al. (2021) showed that the E3SM water budget simulation results are more sensitive to time step when the micro/macro cloud physics is sub cycled together.
    • Pronounced reduction of cloud fraction over stratocumulus regions is known to be mostly due to the timestep sensitivity outside the cloud physics scheme. However, there are still remaining problems due to the changes in subcycling frequency between MAC-MIC over stratocumulus regions (MACMIC_Shorter simulation in Wan et al. (2021) paper).
    • According to Gettelman et al. (2015), cloud liquid increases when the macrophysics and microphysics subcycled more frequently in the CAM5 model (different macrophysics scheme with E3SM).
    • (following Figures are from Wan et al. 2021)

    Note

    • conflicting results in Santos et al. (2021) and Wan et al. (2021).
      • in Santos et al. (2021), high cloud increases in All_Shorter and All_Except_MacMic_Shorter simulation, but decrease in MacMic_shorter simulation.
      • in Wan et al. (2021), high cloud decreases in All_Shorter and MacMic_shorter simulation, but increase in high latitude in All_Except_MacMic_Shorter simulation.

    Note

    • Some evidence suggests that the lack of microphysical sensitivity in shallow convection is associated with an increasing dominance of the accretion process over autoconversion as precipitation rates increase (Stevens and Seifert 2008).
    • In Gettelman et al. (2015), increasing Nc increased accretion and Ac/Au ratio by an order of magnitude.
    • Posselt and Lohmann (2008) show that a prognostic treatment of precipitation increases the relative contribution to precipitation from accretion compared with autoconversion in weakly precipitating clouds. Given that this appears from Wood et al. (2009) model results to be a critical determinant of the sensitivity of precipitation to cloud microphysics, it may be plausible to hypothesize that diagnostic treatment of warm rain formation in climate models will overestimate the microphysical sensitivity and therefore the second AIE.

    • forming more cloud water lead to increase the accretion rate.
      • smaller cloud fraction increment and larger cloud water increment may lead to heavier precipitation (since the accretion & autoconversions are the function of in-cloud cloud water and in-cloud rain):
      • According to Posselt and Lohmann (2008), in the regions where the total water path is smaller (drizzling regions, e.g., stratocumulus region), precipitation amount is sensitive to sub-time steps because liquid water has to accumulate in the atmosphere before conversion processes can produce precipitation.
        • Increasing the number of sub-time steps leads to changes in the autoconversion and accretion rates. More sub-time steps lead to slower sedimentation and thus rain water is kept longer in the atmosphere and is maintained there for the next (model) time step (compared to a diagnostic scheme where all rain water is removed within one time step). Thus, there is generally more rain water in the atmosphere the larger the number of sub-time steps. And more rain water means higher accretion rates. As the accretion process also removes cloud water, less cloud water is available for the autoconversion. Therefore, the autoconversion rates decrease. Thus, the total conversion rate (=autoconversion+accretion rate) increases if the increase in accretion overcompensates the decrease in autoconversion. A decrease of the total conversion rate results if the decrease in autoconversion is larger than the increase in accretion.
        • accretion으로 생긴 강수들은 대체로 sedimentation으로 넘어감.

    variable description

    • \(q_v\) = water vapor mixing ratio
    • \(q_l\) = cloud water mixing ratio
    • \(q_r\) = rain water mixing ratio
    • D = \(q_v\) tendency due to dynamics, turbulence, etc.
    • C = \(q_l\) tendency due to cloud formation (=\(q_v\) tendency due to cloud formation).
    • R = \(q_r\) tendency due to rain formation (=\(q_l\) tendency due to autoconversion/accretion by rain)
    • P = \(q_r\) tendency due to sedimentation.
    • Basic equation
      • \(\frac{dq_v}{dt} = D - C\)
      • \(\frac{dq_l}{dt} = C - R\)
      • \(\frac{dq_r}{dt} = R - P\)
      • The C term and the R term are almost twice as large in macmic36 simulation compared to other simulations.
        • still need to investigate
        • as in Gettelman et al. (2015), \(q_l\) and \(q_r\) are larger when cloud macro-microphysics subcycle more frequently (see B and D)
      • Increase in the C term depletes \(q_v\). Since stratocumulus regions have well-mixed boundary layer, turbulent mixing replenishes the RH and \(q_v\) (Q in the plot) within the boundary layer (D term in the equation increases), but depletes the \(q_v\) above the boundary layer (see vertical integral of Q differences).
        • cloud water near cloud top decreases even when the cloud water tendency increase (see normalized vertically integrated cloud water & tendency above and below the PBL height plot).
        • Q decrease near the surface (vertical profile plots) seems to be mostly due to less evaporation.
          • accretion increases -> rain increases -> rain fall speed (proportional to \(q_r^{0.8}\)) increases -> evaporation decreases (see vertical profile/cloud product plots).
        • RH decrease near cloud top seems to be due to less cloud LW radiative cooling effect.
